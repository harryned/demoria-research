How the index is built

A composite measure of demographic sustainability across 236 countries and territories, calibrated against UN data and grounded in the real-world consequences of population change.

01 — Overview

What the DHI measures and why

Conventional demographic indicators such as total fertility rate, old-age dependency ratio and net migration each describe one aspect of population change. The DHI brings these elements together in a single framework intended to answer a broader question: how strong or fragile are a society's demographic foundations?

Demographic health is not the same thing as simple population growth. A country may record relatively high fertility and still face structural weaknesses in workforce formation, dependency burdens or human capital. Another may have below-replacement fertility but compensate to some extent through migration, labour-force participation or productivity. The DHI is intended to reflect that complexity rather than reduce it to a single variable.

The index primarily draws on data from the United Nations World Population Prospects 2024, using observed data from 1950 to 2024 and median-variant projections to 2050. Scores are calculated for 236 countries and territories from 1965–2050.

Each country receives an overall score between 0 and 100, built from five weighted categories. In 2024, no country scores above 91 and none scores below 22. That distribution suggests that both outright demographic collapse and exceptionally strong demographic resilience are uncommon. Most countries sit somewhere between those extremes, albeit on very different trajectories.

Five categories, 100 points

03 — Scoring

How raw indicators become scores

Each indicator is transformed into a 0–100 sub-score using purpose-built functions that reflect demographic reality rather than mathematical convenience. These functions are deliberately non-symmetrical.

01

Asymmetric scoring curves

Demographic problems do not behave linearly. A total fertility rate of 1.0 represents a structural emergency; a TFR of 2.5 represents modest above-replacement fertility. These are not mirror images. Scoring curves are calibrated to reflect the empirical consequences of different values rather than their arithmetic distance from a midpoint.

02

Sweet spots, not simple maxima

Many metrics have an optimal range rather than a simple "higher is better" rule. Net reproduction rate, for example, scores 100 anywhere between 1.0 and 1.8, reflecting that modest above-replacement reproduction is equivalent to exact replacement for index purposes. Very high NRR (implying both very high fertility and low child mortality) receives a modest penalty.

03

Floor anchoring for visualisation

The overall index has an empirical floor of approximately 22 (Ukraine scores 22.1 in 2024, the lowest of any country). The ceiling is approximately 91 (Uzbekistan, 90.9). Colour scales and visual encodings are anchored at 20 rather than 0, ensuring the full gradient maps across the range of real variation rather than wasting spectrum on scores no country achieves.

04

Context-adjusted migration penalty

The migration dependence penalty is adjusted by each country's migration regime classification: birth-led, migration-sustained, migration-dominated, or in accelerated decline. A country that has always relied on migration for growth (e.g. Gulf states) is penalised less harshly for immigrant stock levels than one undergoing rapid transition toward migration dependence.

05

Projection scenarios

Scores for 2025, 2035, and 2050 use the UN WPP 2024 Median fertility variant. This represents the central estimate and should not be read as a precise forecast. The projection years are shown to indicate structural direction rather than precise outcomes.

04 — Classifications

Five demographic classifications

Overall scores are grouped into five classifications reflecting the severity and character of a country's demographic situation. Thresholds are calibrated against empirical breakpoints in the data rather than equal quantiles.

Classification Score range Countries (2024) Interpretation
Demographically Healthy 80 – 100 57 countries (25%) Broadly self-sustaining fertility, young workforce, low migration dependence. Predominantly Central Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and Melanesia.
Under Pressure 65 – 79 57 countries (25%) Fertility declining but manageable. Workforce ageing beginning. Most of the Middle East, South Asia, and South-East Asia.
Serious Decline 50 – 64 52 countries (23%) Sub-replacement fertility established. Population momentum fading. Migration increasingly compensating. Latin America, parts of South-East Asia.
Critical 35 – 49 51 countries (22%) Cannot replace population without substantial migration. Workforce contraction imminent. Most of Europe, North America, East Asia, including China (49.6) and Denmark (49.5).
Complete Crisis 0 – 34 11 countries (5%) Structural demographic collapse. TFR well below 1.4. Rapidly ageing societies with no realistic path to replacement without transformative policy. Italy, South Korea, Japan, much of Southern and Eastern Europe.

The 50-point threshold is deliberately placed to reflect a meaningful divide: countries above 50 retain at least some organic demographic capacity, even if declining. Countries below 50 are structurally dependent on migration, productivity gains, or policy intervention to maintain population stability. China's 49.6 and Denmark's 49.5 landing on either side of this line reflects genuinely similar structural challenges, despite their very different fertility histories.

05 — Data Sources

Underlying data

All data is sourced from internationally comparable official datasets. No modelled or imputed values are used where official data is available.

UN WPP 2024
World Population Prospects 2024: primary source for fertility, mortality, population structure, net reproduction rate, and all projection-year values. Estimates 1950–2024; Low/Medium/High variants to 2100.
UN DESA Migration Stock
International migrant stock as a percentage of total population, 1990–2024, with projections to 2035 and 2050. Used to compute immigrant stock share and derive migration regime classifications.
World Bank GDP per capita
Log GDP per capita (PPP, constant 2017 international $) used as a contextual control variable in migration regime classification. Not directly included in the index score.

The UN WPP 2024 is the most comprehensive and internally consistent cross-national demographic dataset available, covering 237 countries and territories with standardised methodology. All scoring functions are calibrated against this dataset's empirical distributions.

06 — Limitations & Caveats

What the index does not capture

Any composite index involves simplification. The following limitations should be understood before drawing policy conclusions from DHI scores.

  • The index measures demographic sustainability, not demographic desirability. A high fertility score does not constitute an endorsement of high fertility. The index is descriptive, not normative.
  • Regional aggregations are currently unweighted arithmetic means. This means a region's score is influenced equally by Luxembourg (population 660,000) and Germany (population 84 million). Population-weighted regional scores would produce meaningfully different results.
  • Projection-year scores (2025, 2035, 2050) use the UN Median variant only. The Low and High variants exist for fertility-sensitive metrics but are not yet propagated through the full scoring pipeline. Projection scores should be interpreted as directional signals rather than forecasts.
  • Migration data quality is uneven across countries. Immigrant stock estimates for small island territories and conflict-affected states carry higher uncertainty than those for large OECD economies with robust statistical systems.
  • The index does not capture policy effectiveness, cultural attitudes toward family formation, housing costs, childcare provision, or any of the structural factors that drive fertility decisions. Two countries with the same score may face entirely different policy challenges.
  • The Cohort Replacement Rate (CRR) was included in earlier versions of the index but replaced by the Net Reproduction Rate (NRR) in the current version. NRR provides the same information with a more interpretively clean threshold of 1.0 = exact replacement.
