Why it matters

So What?

Why the index exists — and why it matters for your country.

Demography is not the only force that shapes a society, but it is one of the few that touches almost everything: public finance, healthcare, schools, housing and the long-term stability of the state itself. When demographic strength weakens, the consequences tend to build slowly before arriving with full force. The DHI brings that trajectory into view in a single comparable score.

Pensions & Fiscal Sustainability
When the share of the working-age population declines, a smaller tax base may be asked to support a larger retired population. Countries in the Critical or Complete Crisis bands are therefore more likely to face sustained fiscal strain, particularly where higher productivity, stronger labour-force participation or migration are insufficient to offset the shift.
Healthcare & Social Care
Older populations tend to require more healthcare and long-term support. If the working-age cohort contracts relative to the number of elderly dependants, pressure on care systems, public finances and families is likely to increase, regardless of national income.
Economic Growth & Innovation
Younger and expanding workforces can support consumption, entrepreneurship and the adoption of new technologies. By contrast, demographic decline is often associated with slower growth, softer housing demand and reduced room for public investment, although the scale of these effects differs from one country to another.
Geopolitical Weight
Over the long term, population size and age structure play outsized roles in shaping the military capacity, economic influence and diplomatic reach of countries. A country with weakening demographic fundamentals may find its international position constrained over time, even if that change is not immediately visible. The world's balance of power is constantly shifting, with the huge demographic changes of the 21st century already largely ‘baked-in’.
Communities & Culture
Fertility collapse and population decline change almost everything at the community level. It hollows out towns, closes schools, weakens congregations, erodes civic life and leaves local institutions without the scale needed to endure. What disappears is more than numbers: it is continuity, confidence and the human infrastructure on which communal life depends.
The Window for Response
Demographic momentum means that birth rates today help determine the size of the workforce decades from now. For countries with a falling DHI score, delay makes adjustment more difficult, because the effects of demographic change unfold over long timescales and are not easily reversed.
Methodology

How the index is built

A composite measure of demographic sustainability across 236 countries and territories, calibrated against UN data and grounded in the real-world consequences of population change.

Overview Structure Scoring Classifications Data Limitations

01 — Overview

Conventional demographic indicators such as total fertility rate, old-age dependency ratio and net migration each describe one aspect of population change. The DHI brings these elements together in a single framework intended to answer a broader question: how strong or fragile are a society's demographic foundations?

Demographic health is not the same thing as simple population growth. A country may record relatively high fertility and still face structural weaknesses in workforce formation, dependency burdens or human capital. Another may have below-replacement fertility but compensate to some extent through migration, labour-force participation or productivity. The DHI is intended to reflect that complexity rather than reduce it to a single variable.

The index primarily draws on data from the United Nations World Population Prospects 2024, using observed data from 1950 to 2024 and median-variant projections to 2050. Scores are calculated for 236 countries and territories from 1965–2050.

Each country receives an overall score between 0 and 100, built from five weighted categories. In 2024, no country scores above 91 and none scores below 22.

03 — Scoring

Each indicator is transformed into a 0–100 sub-score using purpose-built functions that reflect demographic reality rather than mathematical convenience.

01

Asymmetric scoring curves

Demographic problems do not behave linearly. A TFR of 1.0 represents a structural emergency; a TFR of 2.5 represents modest above-replacement fertility. Scoring curves are calibrated to reflect empirical consequences rather than arithmetic distance from a midpoint.

02

Sweet spots, not simple maxima

Many metrics have an optimal range rather than a simple "higher is better" rule. Net reproduction rate, for example, scores 100 anywhere between 1.0 and 1.8.

03

Floor anchoring for visualisation

The overall index has an empirical floor of approximately 22 and ceiling of approximately 91. Colour scales are anchored at 20 rather than 0, ensuring the gradient maps across real variation.

04

Context-adjusted migration penalty

The migration dependence penalty is adjusted by each country's migration regime classification: birth-led, migration-sustained, migration-dominated, or in accelerated decline.

05

Projection scenarios

Scores for 2025, 2035, and 2050 use the UN WPP 2024 Median fertility variant. The projection years indicate structural direction rather than precise outcomes.

05 — Data Sources

All data is sourced from internationally comparable official datasets. No modelled or imputed values are used where official data is available.

UN WPP 2024
World Population Prospects 2024: primary source for fertility, mortality, population structure, net reproduction rate, and all projection-year values.
UN DESA Migration Stock
International migrant stock as a percentage of total population, 1990–2024, with projections to 2035 and 2050.
World Bank GDP per capita
Log GDP per capita (PPP, constant 2017 international $) used as a contextual control variable in migration regime classification.

06 — Limitations & Caveats

Any composite index involves simplification.

  • The index measures demographic sustainability, not demographic desirability. The index is descriptive, not normative.
  • Regional aggregations are currently unweighted arithmetic means — Luxembourg and Germany count equally.
  • Projection-year scores (2025, 2035, 2050) use the UN Median variant only and should be read as directional signals rather than forecasts.
  • Migration data quality is uneven across countries; small island territories and conflict-affected states carry higher uncertainty.
  • The index does not capture policy effectiveness, cultural attitudes toward family formation, housing costs, childcare provision, or any of the structural factors that drive fertility decisions.

The DHI does not prescribe a particular policy response. Its purpose is simply to measure the underlying demographic condition of a society and to make that condition visible, comparable and trackable over time.
